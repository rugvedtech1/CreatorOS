# Audience Psychology Knowledge

Reference this file when explaining *why* a content or engagement tactic
works — this skill should always teach the underlying principle, not just
hand over a tactic.

## Attention and scroll behavior

- Viewers decide to keep scrolling within roughly the first second of
  seeing content — this is why hook strength is treated as the single
  highest-leverage part of a script.
- Pattern interrupts (visual or tonal shifts) reset attention because the
  brain is wired to notice change more than continuity — this is why a
  static, unchanging shot loses viewers even if the content itself is
  good.

## Curiosity and open loops

Unresolved questions create psychological tension the brain wants to
resolve (the "curiosity gap"). Opening a loop early ("the mistake that
cost me...") and resolving it later keeps viewers watching to closure.
Overuse without payoff damages trust — the loop must resolve meaningfully.

## Social proof

People look to others' behavior to decide their own, especially under
uncertainty. This is why comment sections and share counts influence how
new viewers perceive content quality — but it means early engagement
(first hour after posting) has outsized importance, which is why prompt
replying (`engines/community-building.md`) compounds.

## Identity and sharing behavior

People share content that reflects who they are or want to be seen as —
"this is so me" or "this is exactly my humor" content spreads through
identity-signaling, not just information value. This is distinct from
save behavior, which is driven by utility/reference value instead.

## Reciprocity

When a creator gives genuine value (a real answer, real help) without
immediately asking for something back, it builds goodwill that later
increases willingness to engage, follow, or buy — this underlies why
value-first content outperforms promotional-first content for long-term
growth.

## Loss aversion vs. gain framing

Framing around what someone might lose/miss out on tends to motivate
action more strongly than framing around equivalent gains — useful for
CTA phrasing, but must be used honestly (real stakes only) to avoid
crossing into manipulative fear-based framing, which this skill avoids.

## Parasocial relationships

Audiences form real one-directional relationships with creators they
watch consistently — this is why BTS/daily-life content
(`engines/story-engine.md`) builds loyalty disproportionate to its
production effort; it feeds the sense of "knowing" the creator personally.

## Consistency and habit formation

Predictable posting rhythms help audiences form a checking-in habit,
which compounds engagement over time independent of any single post's
quality — this is why series/recurring formats
(`engines/community-building.md`) outperform one-off content for
retention.
